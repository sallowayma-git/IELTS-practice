The Tasmanian Tiger

Paragraph A

The thylacine, commonly known as the Tasmanian tiger, was a unique marsupial carnivore that once roamed the Australian continent and the island of Tasmania. Despite its common name, the thylacine was not closely related to tigers or other big cats. Instead, it was a marsupial, more closely related to kangaroos and koalas. The animal was characterized by its distinctive stripes across the lower back, dog-like appearance, and ability to open its jaws at an extraordinary 120-degree angle. For thousands of years, the thylacine was a successful predator in its environment, occupying a similar ecological niche to wolves and other large carnivores.

Paragraph B

Fossil evidence suggests that the thylacine was once widespread across the Australian mainland, with remains dating back millions of years. However, the species began to decline on the mainland approximately 2,000 years ago, disappearing entirely by around 3,000 years ago. The exact reasons for this mainland extinction remain debated among scientists, but several factors likely contributed to the decline. These include climate change, habitat loss, competition with dingoes (which arrived in Australia about 4,000 years ago), and changes in Aboriginal hunting practices. By the time of European settlement in the 18th century, the thylacine was found only in Tasmania.

Paragraph C

When European settlers arrived in Tasmania in the early 19th century, they encountered a thriving thylacine population. However, the relationship between settlers and thylacines quickly became antagonistic. The newcomers brought with them large numbers of sheep and other livestock, which became easy prey for the native predators. As thylacines began to attack sheep, they were perceived as pests and threats to the settlers' livelihoods. This perception was reinforced by the economic importance of the wool industry to Tasmania's developing economy, making the elimination of perceived threats a priority for the colonial government.

Paragraph D

In response to pressure from farmers and graziers, the Tasmanian government introduced a bounty system for thylacine carcasses in 1888. This bounty, which paid rewards for proof of killing a thylacine, remained in effect for nearly four decades. The system was initially intended to control what was believed to be a large and resilient population, but it ultimately proved devastating to the species. Thousands of thylacines were killed during this period, with official records indicating that over 2,000 bounties were paid out between 1888 and 1909 alone. However, the actual number of animals killed was likely much higher, as many kills went unreported or undocumented.

Paragraph E

As the 20th century progressed, it became increasingly clear that the thylacine population was in serious decline. By the 1920s, sightings of the animals had become rare, and concerns about the species' survival began to emerge. In 1936, just two months after the thylacine was finally granted protected status by the Tasmanian government, the last known captive individual died at Hobart Zoo. This animal, named Benjamin, became a symbol of human-caused extinction. The exact date of the species' complete extinction in the wild remains uncertain, but it is believed that the last wild thylacine was killed in the 1930s, making the thylacine yet another victim of human expansion and misunderstanding.

Paragraph F

Since the extinction of the thylacine, there have been numerous reported sightings throughout Tasmania and mainland Australia. These reports have ranged from credible accounts by experienced naturalists to more dubious claims by amateur observers. Despite extensive searches and investigations, no definitive evidence of surviving thylacines has ever been found. The Tasmanian government officially considers the species extinct, and the Australian government lists it as extinct under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act. However, the persistence of sightings and public fascination with the animal has kept hope alive among some researchers and enthusiasts.

Paragraph G

The story of the thylacine has become a cautionary tale about the consequences of human expansion and the vulnerability of isolated populations. Modern genetic studies have revealed that Tasmanian thylacine populations suffered from extremely low genetic diversity, making them particularly susceptible to extinction pressures. This genetic bottleneck likely resulted from the species' isolation on Tasmania following rising sea levels that separated the island from the mainland. The thylacine's extinction serves as a powerful reminder of the need for proactive conservation efforts and the potential for human activities to drive even seemingly robust species to extinction.
