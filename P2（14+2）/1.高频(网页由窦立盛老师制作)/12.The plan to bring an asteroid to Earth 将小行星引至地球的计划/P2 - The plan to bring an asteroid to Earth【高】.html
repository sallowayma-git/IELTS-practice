The plan to bring an asteroid to Earth

Paragraph A

In 2010, NASA announced an ambitious plan to capture a near-Earth asteroid and bring it into lunar orbit, where astronauts could visit it and study it up close. This mission, known as the Asteroid Redirect Mission (ARM), was designed to test technologies and techniques that would be needed for future missions to Mars. The plan involved using a robotic spacecraft to capture a small asteroid or a boulder from a larger asteroid's surface, and then redirecting it into a stable orbit around the Moon. This would create a unique opportunity for scientific research and help prepare for the challenges of deep space exploration.

Paragraph B

The concept of asteroid capture is not entirely new. Scientists have long been interested in asteroids for both scientific and practical reasons. Asteroids are remnants from the early solar system, and studying them can provide valuable insights into the formation of planets and the origins of life on Earth. Additionally, some asteroids contain valuable resources such as metals and water, which could potentially be mined to support space exploration or even brought back to Earth. The ARM would be the first mission to demonstrate the capability to move a large object in space, which could have applications for planetary defense against potentially hazardous asteroids.

Paragraph C

The ARM mission would be carried out in several phases. First, a robotic spacecraft would be launched to rendezvous with a suitable asteroid. The spacecraft would need to match the asteroid's velocity and trajectory, which requires precise navigation and propulsion systems. Once the spacecraft reached the asteroid, it would deploy a large bag-like structure to capture the target. For smaller asteroids, the entire object would be enclosed in the bag, while for larger asteroids, a robotic arm would be used to collect a large boulder from the surface. The spacecraft would then use its solar electric propulsion system to slowly tow the captured asteroid back to the Moon.

Paragraph D

The selection of an appropriate asteroid target was a critical aspect of the mission planning. Scientists identified several candidate asteroids based on their size, composition, and orbital characteristics. The ideal target would be between 7 and 10 meters in diameter, which is large enough to be scientifically interesting but small enough to be moved with current technology. The asteroid would need to be in an orbit that makes it relatively easy to reach, and its composition should be of scientific interest. Additionally, the target should pose no threat to Earth, as the mission's purpose is peaceful exploration rather than planetary defense.

Paragraph E

The spacecraft designed for the ARM mission would incorporate several innovative technologies. The primary propulsion system would be solar electric propulsion, which uses solar panels to generate electricity that powers ion thrusters. These thrusters are much more fuel-efficient than traditional chemical rockets, although they produce less thrust. This makes them ideal for long-duration missions where time is less critical than fuel efficiency. The spacecraft would also feature advanced autonomous navigation systems that would allow it to approach and capture the asteroid with minimal human intervention.

Paragraph F

Once the asteroid was captured and brought into lunar orbit, astronauts would visit it using the Orion spacecraft and the Space Launch System (SLS) rocket. This would be the first deep space mission for astronauts since the Apollo program ended in 1972. The astronauts would conduct spacewalks to explore the asteroid's surface, collect samples, and test tools and techniques that could be used on future Mars missions. The mission would provide valuable experience in deep space operations, including navigation, communication delays, and extravehicular activities in deep space.

Paragraph G

Despite its scientific and technological merits, the ARM faced significant criticism and was ultimately cancelled in 2017. Critics argued that the mission was too expensive and that its objectives could be achieved more efficiently through other means. Some scientists questioned the scientific value of studying a relocated asteroid rather than visiting asteroids in their natural environment. Others felt that the mission did not adequately prepare astronauts for Mars exploration, as the conditions would be significantly different. Budget constraints and changing political priorities also contributed to the decision to cancel the mission.

Paragraph H

Although the ARM was cancelled, many of the technologies and concepts developed for the mission continue to influence space exploration plans. The solar electric propulsion system has been adapted for other missions, and the experience gained in autonomous navigation and asteroid characterization has proven valuable. The mission also demonstrated the feasibility of capturing and moving large objects in space, which could be applied to future planetary defense efforts. Moreover, the concept of using asteroids as stepping stones for deep space exploration remains attractive, and similar missions are being considered by other space agencies around the world.

Paragraph I

The broader implications of asteroid capture extend beyond the specific ARM mission. As space agencies and private companies develop capabilities for deep space exploration, asteroids are increasingly seen as potential destinations and resources. The water and metals found in asteroids could support long-duration space missions by providing fuel, oxygen, and construction materials. This could dramatically reduce the cost and complexity of space exploration by eliminating the need to launch all required materials from Earth. The development of asteroid mining technologies could also have profound economic implications, potentially providing access to rare materials that are becoming scarce on Earth.
