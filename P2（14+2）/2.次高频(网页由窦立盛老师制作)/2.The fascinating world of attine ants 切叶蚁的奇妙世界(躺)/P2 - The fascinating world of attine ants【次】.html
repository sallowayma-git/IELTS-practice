P2 - The fascinating world of attine ants【次】

Reading Passage

Deep in the rainforests of Central and South America, a remarkable agricultural revolution has been taking place for millions of years. Attine ants, also known as leafcutter ants, have developed one of the most sophisticated farming systems in the animal kingdom, cultivating fungus gardens that sustain their entire colonies.

The process begins when worker ants venture out from their underground nests to harvest fresh leaves from the forest canopy. However, these ants do not eat the leaves directly. Instead, they carry the leaf fragments back to their colonies, where they are processed by smaller worker ants who chew them into a pulp-like substance. This masticated material serves as the growing medium for a special type of fungus that the ants have been cultivating for over 50 million years.

The relationship between attine ants and their fungus is a perfect example of mutualism in nature. The ants provide the fungus with fresh organic matter and carefully maintain optimal growing conditions, including temperature and humidity control. In return, the fungus produces specialized structures called gongylidia, which serve as the primary food source for the ant colony. These nutrient-rich structures are perfectly adapted to meet the ants' dietary needs.

What makes this system even more remarkable is the ants' sophisticated pest management strategies. The fungus gardens are constantly threatened by parasitic fungi and bacteria that could destroy the crop. To combat these threats, attine ants have evolved a three-pronged defense system. First, they practice strict hygiene, removing any contaminated material from their gardens. Second, they secrete antimicrobial compounds that help suppress harmful microorganisms. Third, and most fascinatingly, they maintain colonies of beneficial bacteria on their bodies that produce antibiotics specifically targeted against garden parasites.

The complexity of attine ant agriculture rivals that of human farming in many ways. Different species have specialized roles within the colony, from the large soldiers that defend the nest to the tiny workers that tend the fungus gardens with meticulous care. The ants even practice crop rotation, abandoning exhausted garden chambers and establishing new ones to maintain soil fertility.

Recent research has revealed that this ancient partnership has led to remarkable evolutionary adaptations on both sides. The cultivated fungus has become so dependent on the ants that it can no longer survive in the wild, while the ants have lost the ability to digest cellulose directly and rely entirely on their fungal partners for nutrition. This co-evolution represents one of the most successful agricultural partnerships in the natural world.
